# Intelligence-Cockpit HCI for Greyline: Situational-Awareness Display Science, Risk-Color Encoding, Provenance/Confidence Trust UX, and a Command-Palette Pro Workflow

_Part of the Greyline research corpus. Generated from cited 2026 web research; see Sources._

## Executive summary

Greyline's prior HCI research (47 sources) optimized the home page as an editorial *cockpit* in the aesthetic sense — it made the surface look like a professional instrument. This stream supplies the *scientific* backbone of the professional/intelligence genre that turns that aesthetic into a defensible, sellable situational-awareness tool. The throughline is that "looks like a cockpit" and "behaves like a cockpit" are different claims, and only the second one survives expert scrutiny or justifies a professional tier.

Endsley's Situation Awareness (SA) model — perceive, comprehend, project — and its formally reviewed 2024-2025 SA-Oriented-Design (SAOD) methodology give Greyline a testable design contract: every screen should be auditable for whether it raises or lowers SA, must explicitly flag missing or unreliable data, and must support Level-3 *projection* (trend/forecast), not merely Level-1 display. Ecological Interface Design (EID) studies across aviation, nuclear, and process control consistently show EID-style interfaces cut workload while holding or improving SA, with the largest gains precisely under stress and in unanticipated situations — exactly Greyline's threat-prep use case. The alarm-management canon (EEMUA 191 ed.4 2024, ANSI/ISA-18.2-2016, IEC 62682) provides hard anti-alarm-fatigue numbers — roughly 6-12 alerts/hr/operator, under 5% high-priority, controlled and auditable shelving with handover review — that should govern Greyline's advisory/weather/disaster alerts so the product never cries wolf.

On the encoding side, Cleveland-McGill's perceptual-accuracy hierarchy and Munzner's channel-effectiveness principle argue for position and length over area and angle for quantitative risk, and the accessibility literature (Petroff's CVD-safe sequences plus ColorBrewer) condemns red/green traffic-light risk encoding. Greyline already ships a semantic Tone system (good/caution/warn/danger) and a map `ALERT_COLOR` Red/Orange/Green table; both should be re-grounded on a blue-orange-crimson, CVD-tested, redundant-cue (icon + label + position) scale. For trust and rigor, ICD 203 supplies a ready-made, citable vocabulary: standardized words-of-estimative-probability bands, the rule that confidence level must never be mixed with likelihood in one sentence, and source-quality summaries — a perfect spine for confidence and provenance chips. The C2PA provenance study warns that broken or missing provenance must be designed as carefully as valid provenance, or users disbelieve honest data.

Genre exemplars — Palantir's object-view/timeline/graph patterns, Kepler.gl/deck.gl brushing and GPU layers on MapLibre, Bloomberg's density-with-concealment, FlightRadar24's tap-to-reveal plus icon-shape encoding plus filter-to-de-crowd, and Liveuamap's regional layering — together with the command-palette canon (Superhuman/Linear/cmdk) translate into five concrete upgrades: a true SA cockpit with projection, a re-grounded risk-color system, an investigation/case timeline, confidence-plus-provenance chips, and a keyboard-first pro workflow. All are buildable offline-first on Greyline's existing Next.js / MapLibre / SQLite stack with no new data model and, in most cases, no new network dependency.

## Key findings

### Situational awareness as a design contract

**SA-Oriented Design is now a formally reviewed methodology, and it imposes enforceable rules.** Endsley & Jones' SAOD review was accepted in the *International Journal of Human-Computer Interaction* (Feb 2024), alongside the 3rd edition of the book (2025) ([tandfonline.com](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/10447318.2024.2318884)). Its core, enforceable rule for Greyline: any new feature must first be evaluated for whether it raises or lowers situation awareness, and displays must (a) explicitly identify missing or unreliable information, (b) support rapid global SA during an "alarm state," and (c) support Level-3 projection of future states (trend/forecast), not just current values. Greyline today shows mostly Level-1/2 — current advisory, current weather — and almost no Level-3 projection, which is precisely the level SAOD says most improves decisions under stress.

**EID empirically reduces workload while preserving or improving SA, and the benefit is largest under stress and in unanticipated situations.** An aviation risk-interface EID study (n=23) found higher usability and lower workload with large effect sizes and no loss of decision accuracy or SA versus an airline's production interface ([researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/356210024_Clear_and_present_danger_Applying_ecological_interface_design_to_develop_an_aviation_risk_management_interface)). Nuclear and process-control studies show EID's SA advantage concentrated in the detection phases without procedural support. This is direct evidence that a more abstraction-hierarchy-driven Greyline threat display would help most exactly when a traveler is in a degraded, high-stress environment — the moment the product is supposed to earn its keep.

### Alarm management and anti-alarm-fatigue budgets

**The alarm-management standards give Greyline concrete, citable anti-alarm-fatigue budgets.** EEMUA 191 (4th ed., Nov 2024) targets roughly 6 alarms/hr (max ~12) per operator, under 5% high-priority, and near-zero standing alarms; an alarm "flood" is defined as more than 10 new alarms per 10 minutes, and a "chattering" alarm trips more than 5 times in 60 seconds ([eemua.org](https://www.eemua.org/getattachment/9d3f8071-55c3-49bf-a74a-3bf6ad4a2e0f/Contents-EEMUA-Publication-191-Edition4-November-2024.pdf)). Suppression must be controlled, visible, and audited — who shelved what, why, for how long, and reviewed at shift handover. Greyline's stacked advisory/weather/disaster/earthquake layers risk exactly this fatigue and currently lack rate-limiting, dedup, or an audited mute/shelve mechanism.

**The lifecycle discipline behind those numbers is rationalize-before-you-alert.** ANSI/ISA-18.2-2016 defines the alarm lifecycle (philosophy → rationalization → ... → audit) and the controlled-suppression categories — Designed, Shelved, Out-of-Service ([18817087.s21i.faiusr.com](https://18817087.s21i.faiusr.com/61/ABUIABA9GAAgyZfj5AUozIu7wwI.pdf)). For Greyline this means no signal should become an alert until it has been rationalized against a defined user action, and any suppression must land in one of the named, auditable states rather than a silent on/off toggle.

### Quantitative encoding and CVD-safe risk color

**Cleveland-McGill plus Munzner dictate that magnitude belongs to position/length and color belongs to category.** Cleveland & McGill's empirical ranking (JASA 1984) shows position and aligned length are decoded most accurately, while area, angle, and color are error-prone for quantitative magnitude ([tandfonline.com](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/01621459.1984.10478080)); Munzner's effectiveness principle extends this. Greyline should encode magnitude — days-abroad, minimum-connection-time risk minutes, advisory level — with position/length bars and reserve color for category or status, not the reverse. Cleveland's maxim, "a visualization fails if the decoding fails," is the acceptance test.

**Red/green traffic-light risk encoding is an accessibility failure, and the literature converges on a specific fix.** Roughly 1 in 12 men have a color-vision deficiency. The convergent fix: blue-orange (not red-green) palettes, ALWAYS paired with a redundant non-color cue — shape, icon, pattern, label, or position — validated by a grayscale test and WCAG contrast (3:1 for graphics, 4.5:1 for text), using CVD-safe sets. ColorBrewer colorblind-safe palettes and Petroff's 2021 accessible sequences (built with minimum-perceptual-distance, CVD-simulation, and grayscale-lightness constraints) are the concrete sources ([arxiv.org/pdf/2107.02270](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2107.02270); [express.excelsior.edu](https://express.excelsior.edu/datascience/chapter/5-4-color-theory-and-accessibility-in-data-visualization/)). Greyline already has a semantic Tone enum (good/caution/warn/danger/neutral → text-success/warning/destructive in `lib/intel.ts`) and a map `ALERT_COLOR` Red/Orange/Green table in `map-view.tsx` — both should be re-grounded on a CVD-tested scale with mandatory icon/label redundancy.

### Trust vocabulary: confidence, likelihood, and provenance

**ICD 203 is a ready-made, authoritative trust vocabulary Greyline can adopt nearly verbatim.** The ODNI Analytic Standards give standardized words-of-estimative-probability bands — almost no chance 1-5%, very unlikely 5-20%, unlikely 20-45%, roughly even 45-55%, likely 55-80%, very likely 80-95%, almost certain 95-99% — plus the hard rule that a "confidence level" (e.g., high confidence) must NOT be combined with a degree of likelihood in the same sentence, and ICD 206 source-quality summary statements ([intelligence.gov ICD 203 PDF](https://www.intelligence.gov/assets/documents/intelligence-community-directives/ICD_203.pdf); content cross-checked against the [wesinator/ICD203 mirror](https://github.com/wesinator/ICD203-intel-analysis) because the official PDF returned 403 to the fetcher). This separates "how sure" (confidence in the source base) from "how likely" (the event) — the exact distinction Greyline's curated intel and advisories need to look rigorous rather than hand-wavy. Verify the extracted band table against the ODNI PDF before shipping copy.

**Provenance UX research shows that broken or missing provenance is as load-bearing as valid provenance.** The UW/Adobe/BBC study (arXiv 2303.12118) found that C2PA-style provenance lowered trust in manipulated media AND that incomplete or invalid provenance made users disbelieve HONEST media; users also conflate media credibility with provenance credibility ([arxiv.org/abs/2303.12118](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.12118)). The implication for Greyline: source and confidence chips must clearly distinguish valid vs. stale vs. missing provenance, and a missing or stale source must always be paired with a next action ("Update advisory" / "Source last fetched 14 d ago"). Because Greyline's offline-first model makes staleness the norm, degraded-provenance design is mission-critical, not an edge case.

### Genre exemplars: object hubs, dense maps, density-with-restraint

**Palantir Gotham/Foundry's documented UX gives Greyline three reusable, ontology-grounded patterns.** From the vendor docs: (1) the Object View as a single biographical hub per entity — Greyline already has country/trip/destination as natural object types; (2) a Timeline widget with grouped lanes, prominent-vs-hidden property tiers, and multiple date properties per event (planned vs. actual) — ideal for a counter-surveillance/case timeline; (3) Object Explorer's "search-around" graph for link exploration (sightings → locations → trips) ([palantir.com/docs](https://www.palantir.com/docs/foundry/object-explorer/getting-started)). These map onto Greyline's existing trips/destinations/sightings tables with no new data model.

**Kepler.gl now runs on the same MapLibre base Greyline uses, making a denser map a drop-in.** Kepler.gl runs on MapLibre GL + deck.gl and adds GPU layers (hexbin aggregation, arcs/flows, heatmaps for 100k+ points) plus "brushing" (radius highlight under the cursor) and a component-injection system for custom UI — all MIT-licensed and self-hostable with local tiles. deck.gl (~13k stars) and kepler.gl (~10.9k stars; Kepler 3.1 added DuckDB) are practical building blocks for a denser Greyline map ([github.com/keplergl/kepler.gl](https://github.com/keplergl/kepler.gl)). Greyline's PMTiles dependency is installed-but-unused, so a bundled offline basemap plus a deck.gl overlay is the missing piece for an offline-first GEOINT surface.

**Information-density exemplars converge on the same principles Greyline can apply.** Bloomberg conceals complexity while pushing high-contrast, "in your face" values and prioritizing keyboard control over discoverability — "closer to a cockpit than a gallery" ([bloomberg.com/company/stories](https://www.bloomberg.com/company/stories/how-bloomberg-terminal-ux-designers-conceal-complexity/)). FlightRadar24 (per the Bellingcat toolkit) uses tap-to-reveal progressive disclosure, category-by-icon-shape (pre-attentive), and filtering to cure icon crowding, plus explicit data-reliability caveats in conflict zones ([bellingcat.gitbook.io](https://bellingcat.gitbook.io/toolkit/more/all-tools/flightaware)). Liveuamap demonstrates regional/topic layering of dense incident feeds. Greyline's MapView already does tap-to-reveal and layer toggles but uses generic dots/glyphs and lacks density management (clustering, filter-to-de-crowd) and pre-attentive icon-shape encoding of marker category.

### Command palette and dark data-dense genre

**The command palette is now a baseline pro-workflow expectation, not a differentiator — but how it is built still differentiates.** Superhuman, Linear, Raycast, and Vercel have made Cmd+K table stakes; cmdk is the de-facto React primitive. Superhuman's own engineering laws: the palette should TEACH shortcuts (show the keybinding every time so users graduate to direct invocation), use optimistic/instant UI with undo as the safety net, and follow the Cmd+K convention ([blog.superhuman.com](https://blog.superhuman.com/how-to-build-a-remarkable-command-palette/)). Greyline's prior memory references a CommandPalette component on the old SvelteKit main branch; the Next.js rewrite should rebuild it as the spine of a keyboard-first investigator workflow — jump to country/trip, run a tool, toggle a connector, place a rally point.

**The highest-value first build is a case timeline plus confidence/provenance layer, not routing.** Greyline's `saved_routes` table is schema-only with no UI, and the stack has no routing/isochrone engine. The EID/SA literature plus the alarm-flood and case-timeline patterns suggest the higher-value first build is a case timeline plus a confidence/provenance layer over data already in SQLite (sightings, advisories, trips) — fully offline, needing no new connectors.

**Dark, data-dense dashboards are the dominant 2025-2026 award/genre style.** Awwwards dark-mode and data-viz collections, plus Tremor (open-sourced under Vercel in 2025 as a dark-first data-dashboard component set built on Recharts), define the genre. It rewards a real-time / near-real-time feel, role/context-tailored views, and high-contrast chart colors on dark — all consistent with Greyline's Oak & Gold dark editorial system, provided color stays CVD-safe and redundant.

## Open questions

1. Should Greyline adopt ICD 203's estimative-probability bands verbatim (authoritative, citable) or a softened plain-language variant, given its civilian traveler/journalist audience may misread "almost certain 95-99%" as overconfident for stale offline data?
2. What is the minimum viable offline basemap size for a bundled PMTiles extract that still feels "premium" globally — a low-zoom worldwide Protomaps extract vs. on-demand per-destination tile bundles (ties to the existing `offline_bundles` table)?
3. Does deck.gl's bundle weight (a real cost flagged in DESIGN.md's native-first principle) justify itself, or can the density upgrades (clustering, brushing) be achieved with MapLibre-native layers + supercluster alone to stay lean?
4. For the alert layer, what counts as a "departure-affecting" high-priority change for a self-directed traveler (no dispatcher) — and should priority thresholds be user-tunable per trip threat tier?
5. Should the case timeline support manual evidence/note attachment (photos via the EXIF stripper, links) to fully serve the journalism pillar, and if so how does that interact with the encrypted vault for sensitive case material?
6. Is there appetite for a formal SA evaluation (a lightweight SAGAT-style self-check or task-completion timing) to validate the cockpit redesign against the prior home page, per Endsley's "evaluate every change for its SA effect" mandate?
7. How should confidence/provenance chips degrade gracefully in the SF-86-grade disclosure export (a legal-ish document) where overstated confidence could be a liability?
