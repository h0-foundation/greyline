# Professional Travel-Risk / Duty-of-Care Competitive Landscape: Positioning Greyline as a Paid, Privacy-First TRM Product

_Part of the Greyline research corpus. Generated from cited 2026 web research; see Sources._

## Executive summary

The professional travel-risk-management (TRM) market splits cleanly into three layers. At the top sit the **full-service assistance + SOC providers** — International SOS (estimated ~$50K+/yr), Crisis24 (~$40K+/yr), Healix, and Global Guardian — who bundle 24/7 human operations centers and medical evacuation. Below them are the **intelligence / risk-scoring feeds** — Riskline (~$10K+/yr), Dataminr ($20K–$100K+/yr), GeoSure (partner-API-only), and A3M — who sell country/city risk scores and event alerting. At the bottom is the **traveler-UX / itinerary layer** — TripIt Pro ($49/yr), Navan, TravelPerk, and Sherpa's visa/requirements API ($1.5K–$5K/mo). Almost none publish prices; the entire category is sold on the language of "duty of care," ISO 31030:2021 compliance, proprietary risk scores, real-time traveler tracking on a live map, and 24/7 human assistance.

Two of those pillars are structurally impossible for a local-only product to replicate: the **24/7 SOC + medevac** and the **live GPS traveler tracking**. That is not a weakness to paper over — those are precisely the components that make the incumbents cloud-dependent, surveillance-heavy, and expensive. They require always-on infrastructure and continuous location exfiltration, and they create exactly the subpoena, breach, and single-point-of-compromise exposure that Greyline's target buyers are trying to avoid. Greyline should reframe their absence as the privacy posture it deliberately maintains, not a gap it is hiding.

Everything else buyers actually pay for is **computable offline from bundleable open data**. A defensible composite country/city risk score can be built from the Global Peace Index (163 countries), the Fragile States Index (178 countries), ACLED conflict events, and the CPI / RSF / REST Countries data already in Greyline's database. A pre-trip risk briefing, an itinerary risk timeline mapped to ISO 31030's before/during/after lifecycle, a visa/document engine (Greyline already ships ~50k visa pairs vs Sherpa's paid API), and a journalist-grade risk-assessment + check-in + proof-of-life pack are all expressible over local primitives Greyline already has — a 54-item OPSEC checklist, an encrypted vault, and the `lib/intel.ts`, `lib/trip-briefing.ts`, and `lib/trip-kit.ts` wiring. **The gap is packaging, not capability.**

The local-only / AGPL stance is a genuine, concrete differentiator for the exact buyers who distrust cloud SaaS: investigative journalists, NGOs operating in hostile states, IP-sensitive corporate and legal travelers, activists, and high-net-worth individuals. For these users, putting itineraries, locations, and risk profiles in a vendor's subpoena-able SOC database is itself the threat. The credible "professional" wedge is therefore a self-contained, auditable, ISO-31030-aligned TRM workbench you run on your own machine — "duty of care without surveillance." Monetization that survives AGPL + local-first is a known, proven stack (paid signed desktop build, data-bundle subscription, dual commercial/self-host license, commercial-license-gated Pro feature pack) and is documented below for reference only, deferred behind product validation.

## Key findings

### Pricing and market opacity

**Pricing across the category is almost universally hidden and quote-based, and the opacity is itself an exploitable market gap.** The only public anchors are TripIt Pro at $49/yr (consumer, [tripit.com/web/pro/pricing](https://www.tripit.com/web/pro/pricing)); Sherpa° at $1,500 / $3,000 / $5,000 per month for its Economy / Business / First visa-and-requirements API tiers ([joinsherpa.com](https://www.joinsherpa.com/pricing/travel-restrictions-pricing)); and Region Alert's transparent $5,988/yr, positioned explicitly as the anti-Dataminr ([regionalert.com](https://regionalert.com/blog/travel-risk-management-companies-2026.html)). Third-party and vendor-published estimates put International SOS at $50K+/yr, Crisis24 at $40K+/yr, Everbridge at $15K+, WorldAware at $20K+, Healix at $30K+, Riskline at $10K+, and Dataminr at $20K–$100K+. These numbers are vendor-biased and should be treated as estimates, but they are the only consolidated price table available. Greyline can exploit the opacity directly with honest, fixed, local-first pricing.

### What Greyline structurally cannot replicate

**Two capabilities are genuinely off the table for a no-cloud / no-SOC product, and they should not be faked.** The first is 24/7 human assistance, medical evacuation, and on-the-ground response — the ISOS / Crisis24 model, embodied in Crisis24's CAP one-touch ASSIST button. The second is real-time GPS traveler tracking on a live map — Navan's live map and Safeture's push alerts ([navan.com/blog/corporate-travel-safety](https://navan.com/blog/corporate-travel-safety)). Both require always-on infrastructure and continuous location exfiltration, which is antithetical to local-first. The correct strategic move is not to approximate them but to reframe them as exactly the privacy liabilities Greyline avoids — the surveillance and centralization that the target buyers are paying a premium to escape.

### Risk scoring as the core monetized artifact

**Country/city risk scoring is the single most monetized "premium-feeling" artifact in the category, and every incumbent ships it as an opaque black box.** GeoSure's GeoSafeScores rate locations 1–100 across seven categories — Physical Harm, Health/Medical, Women's Safety, LGBTQ+, Theft, Political Freedoms, and a Day/Night Adjuster — across 65k–400k locations, available only as a partner API with no public pricing, and powering Concur/TripIt/Amadeus ([geosure.ai/api](https://geosure.ai/api)). Riskline uses an impact × threat × likelihood model across three domains (security/health/travel) with an Extreme/High/Medium scale drawn from 100k+ sources ([riskline.com/2026-risk-map](https://riskline.com/2026-risk-map/)). International SOS publishes dual medical + security ratings across 240 countries ([internationalsos.com](https://www.internationalsos.com/services/travel-risk-management)). Greyline can compute a transparent, open-methodology composite score offline from the Global Peace Index (163 countries, CSV), the Fragile States Index (178 countries), ACLED conflict events, and its existing CPI / RSF / REST Countries data. "Open, auditable methodology" beats the competitors' proprietary black boxes as a selling point — particularly for journalists and NGOs who must defend their assessments to an editor, a security manager, or a court.

### ISO 31030 as the credibility hook

**ISO 31030:2021 (Travel Risk Management — Guidance for Organizations) is the credibility hook every serious vendor cites, and Greyline can claim alignment legally and accurately.** It is guidance, not a certifiable standard, and it defines a lifecycle: organizational preparedness → preparing for travel → during travel → post-travel → review/audit ([iso.org/standard/54204.html](https://www.iso.org/standard/54204.html)). Greyline can structure its trip pipeline and OPSEC checklist explicitly around these five phases and market itself as "ISO 31030-aligned." That phrasing is legally accurate because the standard is non-certifiable, and it immediately reads professional to corporate and legal buyers. (See Open questions on the precise wording, since vendors are warned not to imply that ISO certification exists.)

### Existing feature parity — the gap is packaging

**Greyline already has feature parity or better on every non-SOC value prop the incumbents charge for.** It ships ~50k visa pairs (versus Sherpa's $1.5K–5K/mo paid API), multi-government advisories (US State Department + UK FCDO), and country dossiers with curated intelligence that no competitor surfaces — VPN legality, decryption compulsion, SIM registration, LGBTQ risk, APIS/PNR, and biometric entry. It has a 54-item threat-tiered OPSEC checklist, an SF-86-grade disclosure export, and `lib/intel.ts` + `lib/trip-briefing.ts` + `lib/trip-kit.ts` already wiring these together. The strategic conclusion is direct: the work remaining is to package these into the recognizable professional shapes (Risk Score, briefing PDF, ISO-31030 timeline), not to build new core capability.

### Privacy as a concrete selling point

**The privacy-first stance is a concrete selling point for a specific, identifiable buyer set — not merely an ideological position.** Investigative journalists (RSF / ACOS / Foley all emphasize that a journalist's itinerary and risk profile are themselves sensitive), NGOs in hostile states, corporate legal and IP-sensitive travelers, activists, and HNWIs all share the same exposure: putting trips, locations, and risk profiles in a cloud SOC creates subpoena exposure, breach risk, and a single point of compromise. "Your dossier never leaves your machine" is a feature ISOS and Crisis24 structurally cannot match, because their entire value model depends on centralizing exactly that data ([rsf.org journalist safety guide](https://rsf.org/en/rsf-acos-medill-foley-foundation-launch-safety-guide-journalists)).

### Journalist / NGO field-safety as an open niche

**Journalist and NGO field-safety workflows are a near-empty, high-utility niche with ready-made, citable templates.** The RSF Safety Guide (in print since 1992), the ACOS Safety Checklist, the James W. Foley Journalist Safety Guide, the Rory Peck Trust risk-assessment template, and the CPJ Security Assessment form all treat a written risk assessment, a check-in plan, and a proof-of-life protocol as the baseline freelancer/NGO safety artifact ([rsf.org](https://rsf.org/en/rsf-acos-medill-foley-foundation-launch-safety-guide-journalists)). These map cleanly onto Greyline's checklist + vault primitives and onto ISO 31030's lifecycle. No consumer or SMB product packages "written risk assessment + check-in plan + proof-of-life protocol" as a first-class artifact, leaving the niche wide open.

### Bundleable open data satisfies offline-first

**Bundleable open datasets exist for nearly every premium feature, satisfying the offline-first constraint — with one critical licensing caveat.** The Global Peace Index (Institute for Economics & Peace, CSV via QoG DataFinder / Statbase, 163 countries, 23 indicators, 3 domains, 2008–2025) and the Fragile States Index (Fund for Peace, 178 countries, downloadable) form the open backbone for an offline composite score ([datafinder.qog.gu.se/dataset/gpi](https://datafinder.qog.gu.se/dataset/gpi)). FEMA's National Risk Index (natural hazard, OpenFEMA) adds the natural-hazard dimension, on top of the visa/airport/advisory packs Greyline already bundles via `scripts/build-*.ts`. **The caveat: ACLED requires a myACLED account, mandates attribution, and PROHIBITS redistribution** ([acleddata.com/eula](https://acleddata.com/eula)) — so it must be an opt-in per-user connector that fetches into the local DB, never a pre-bundled file in the repo.

### Consumer safety apps — only the offline-numbers pattern is replicable

**Consumer "personal safety" apps are almost all cloud or contact-network dependent, with one bundleable exception.** Noonlight (silent hold-and-release to 911), bSafe (voice SOS + live stream), and Life360 all depend on continuous connectivity and a watcher network. The exception is TripWhistle, whose entire value prop is an offline emergency-numbers database for 200+ countries — trivially bundleable, cheap, high-trust, and a feature Greyline currently lacks. That offline-emergency-numbers pattern is the one consumer-app idea worth adopting precisely because it does not require the cloud-tracking that makes the others privacy-hostile.

### Monetization that survives AGPL + local-first (deferred, reference only)

**A proven monetization stack exists for an AGPL local-first app, and is recorded here for reference only — deferred behind product validation.** Keep the AGPL core free; then sell (a) a signed / notarized paid desktop build (Tauri/Electron) so non-technical buyers can skip `pnpm`/`docker`; (b) a data-bundle subscription — monthly-refreshed risk-index, advisory, visa, and conflict packs delivered as signed files, generating recurring revenue without ever hosting user data; (c) a dual commercial / team self-host license for organizations on the Metabase/Budibase pattern at roughly $500–$5K/seat-yr, which also relieves AGPL obligations for orgs ([metabase.com/license/agpl](https://www.metabase.com/license/agpl)); and (d) a commercial-license-gated Pro feature pack (org checklist templating, multi-traveler roster, branded PDF risk-report export). **Avoid SSPL or relicensing "bait-and-switch," which burns community trust.**

## Open questions

- **Primary paying buyer.** Is it solo high-risk individuals (journalists/activists/HNWIs), small NGOs/newsrooms, or SMBs without an ISOS contract? This determines whether to lead with the journalism pack, the team self-host license, or the consumer Pro build.
- **Paid signed desktop build.** Is a paid signed/notarized desktop build (Tauri/Electron wrapper) acceptable given the AGPL core, and who maintains code-signing/notarization across macOS/Windows/Linux? It is the lowest-friction monetization but adds release-ops burden.
- **ACLED connector UX.** ACLED forbids redistribution and requires a myACLED account — is an opt-in per-user connector (each user supplies their own key) acceptable UX, or should the conflict layer rely only on freely-redistributable sources (GDELT, already in Greyline) to keep zero-config offline?
- **Refresh cadence and delivery.** How frequently must bundled risk indices (GPI annual, FSI annual, advisories ~weekly, visa rules ~hourly upstream) refresh to credibly claim "current," and is a signed data-bundle subscription the delivery mechanism versus opt-in proxy connectors?
- **ISO 31030 wording.** Does claiming "ISO 31030-aligned" need legal review to avoid implying certification (vendors are warned not to imply ISO certification exists)? Confirm the exact wording.
- **Open methodology governance.** Should Greyline publish a defended, versioned risk-scoring methodology doc (peer-reviewable) to make "open methodology" a real differentiator, and who owns/maintains the weighting choices?
- **Declared-itinerary sufficiency.** For the team tier, is declared-itinerary aggregation (no GPS) actually sufficient for buyers conditioned to expect live tracking, or does the absence of real-time location become a deal-breaker needing explicit reframing in sales materials?
