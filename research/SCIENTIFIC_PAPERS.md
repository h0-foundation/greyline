# Scientific Foundations for Greyline: A Literature Review Across Situational Awareness, Attention, Mobility Privacy, Surveillance Efficacy, Traveler-Harm Epidemiology, and Data-Visualization Perception

_Part of the Greyline research corpus. Generated from cited 2026 web research; see Sources._

## Executive summary

Greyline's three pillars — travel intelligence, personal security / OPSEC, and investigative-journalism workflows — can be grounded in a mature, citable peer-reviewed literature rather than tradecraft folklore. Five research clusters are directly load-bearing, and each maps to a feature decision the product can defend to a skeptical reviewer with a primary source.

The first two clusters concern the operator's mind. Situational-awareness and naturalistic-decision research — Endsley's three-level SA model (perception → comprehension → projection, _Human Factors_ 1995) and Klein's recognition-primed decision (RPD) model (_Sources of Power_ 1998) — establish that under stress experts act on pattern recognition and a single pre-rehearsed course of action, not on analytic option-comparison. This argues that Greyline's surveillance and OPSEC surfaces should be staged as SA levels and expressed as "if-this-then-that" decision cards, not branching decision trees. The attention literature reinforces this: Simons & Chabris (_Perception_ 1999) measured roughly 46-50% inattentional blindness, which is the empirical backbone of the gray-man thesis, while Sweller's cognitive-load theory explains why decision quality collapses when working memory is overloaded — a direct constraint on the cockpit/home page.

The next two clusters concern the adversary and the threat environment. Mobility-privacy research — de Montjoye et al. (_Scientific Reports_ 2013) — proved that 4 spatio-temporal points re-identify 95% of 1.5M people and that uniqueness decays only as the 1/10 power of spatial resolution, meaning Greyline can compute a re-identification (unicity) risk score on the user's _own_ trip log entirely client-side, and that coarsening grids barely helps. Trajectory-anonymization surveys (k-anonymity, local differential privacy, synthetic generation, 2023-24) supply concrete offline sanitization algorithms. On surveillance efficacy and bias, NIST FRVT Part 3 / NISTIR 8280 (Grother et al. 2019) documented false-positive demographic differentials of 10x to over 100x, grounding evidence-based, non-paranoid threat education; SeaGlass (UW 2017) and EFF's Crocodile Hunter define a detectable IMSI-catcher anomaly model with honest failure modes; and PETS 2024 "Please Unstalk Me" (N=5,253) established a 19% lifetime stalking prevalence with AirTags dominant, supporting a BLE-tracker awareness module.

The final cluster reframes risk and dictates presentation. The CDC Yellow Book (2019-2021 US State Dept data) shows motor-vehicle crashes are the #1 nonnatural traveler death (~26%, 402 deaths) versus only 15 terrorism deaths — the single most important evidence correction Greyline should encode: surface road safety, not terrorism, as the dominant risk. Cleveland & McGill (_JASA_ 1984) supply the perception-ranked encoding rules (position > length > angle > area > color) for every chart and for the scratch-map. Bellingcat's sun/shadow chronolocation method rounds out the journalism workflow with bundleable SunCalc-class solar-ephemeris computation. Taken together, these let Greyline market itself as evidence-based, with each feature traceable to a primary source.

## Key findings

### Situational awareness and naturalistic decision-making

**Experts under stress pattern-match a single workable action rather than comparing options.** Klein's recognition-primed decision (RPD) model — derived from fireground commanders across 156 decision points (_Sources of Power_, 1998; RPD model, Klein, Calderwood & Clinton-Cirocco) — shows experts recognize a situation as typical, retrieve one plausible course of action, and mentally simulate it rather than enumerate and score alternatives ([researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/235418838_A_Recognition_Primed_Decision_RPD_Model_of_Rapid_Decision_Making)). Combined with Endsley's three-level situation-awareness model — perception of cues, comprehension of their meaning, projection of future state (_Human Factors_ 1995, 37(1):32-64; [journals.sagepub.com](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1518/001872095779049543)) — this implies Greyline's OPSEC and counter-surveillance UI should be staged as perception → comprehension → projection and should offer single pre-rehearsed courses of action ("left of bang" decision cards), not multi-option decision trees. Endsley's model further identifies attention and working memory as the limiting factors on SA, tying this cluster directly to the cognitive-load findings below.

### Attention and cognitive load

**Inattentional blindness is real, large, and quantified — observers genuinely miss the unremarkable.** Simons & Chabris (_Perception_ 1999, 28(9):1059-1074; [journals.sagepub.com](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1068/p281059)) found that roughly 46% (up to ~50%) of observers fail to notice an unexpected gorilla walking through a scene, and that noticing depends on task difficulty and target-distractor similarity. This is the empirical backbone of the gray-man thesis: it argues Greyline should frame blending as being low-salience and high-similarity to the local baseline — not "invisible" — because attention is captured by salience and freed by similarity to expected distractors.

**Cognitive load degrades decision quality precisely under time pressure.** Sweller's cognitive-load theory (Sweller, _Cognitive Science_ 1988, 12(2):257-285; summarized at [instructionaldesign.org](https://www.instructionaldesign.org/theories/cognitive-load/)) holds that working memory is severely capacity-limited and that intrinsic, extraneous, and germane load compete for it; when total load exceeds capacity, operators satisfice into hazardous choices. This directly constrains Greyline's cockpit/home page: minimize extraneous load, chunk content into schemas, and surface only decision-relevant cues. It also reinforces the SA/RPD finding — branching decision trees overload working memory exactly when it is scarcest.

### Mobility privacy and re-identification

**Mobility re-identification is near-total and robust to resolution coarsening.** de Montjoye, Hidalgo, Verleysen & Blondel (_Scientific Reports_ 2013, 3:1376; [nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep01376)) analyzed 1.5M individuals over 15 months and showed that 4 random spatio-temporal points uniquely identify 95% of people, 2 points identify more than 50%, and uniqueness decays only as the 1/10 power of spatial and temporal resolution — so coarsening grids barely helps. The practical consequence for Greyline is decisive: it can compute a "unicity score" on the user's own destinations, trips, and route log entirely offline and warn before any export or share. (Figures corroborated by PubMed PMID 23524645 and the MIT DSpace mirror.)

**Trajectory anonymization has concrete, bundleable, client-side algorithms — but no single one is sufficient alone.** The 2023-24 survey literature, including Fiore et al.'s "Privacy in trajectory micro-data publishing" ([arxiv.org/pdf/1903.12211](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1903.12211)), covers k-anonymity (semantic, social, and Voronoi / road-network dummy generation), l-diversity, local differential privacy (e.g., LDPTrace), and synthetic trajectory generation; all of these run client-side. Critically, k-anonymity alone is vulnerable to background-knowledge and continuous-observation attacks, so the documented best practice for any Greyline export-sanitization feature is a hybrid k-anonymity + differential-privacy approach.

**Greyline's existing schema already holds the raw material for an evidence-based privacy score.** The `counter_surveillance_log` (TEDD / repeat-pattern data), `trips` + `destinations` (per-country day totals — the SF-86 disclosure window), and `saved_routes` together constitute exactly the spatio-temporal point set de Montjoye's unicity metric consumes. The data exists; only the analytics layer is missing.

### Surveillance efficacy and demographic bias

**Face-recognition error is large and demographically skewed, which grounds non-paranoid threat education.** NIST FRVT Part 3 / NISTIR 8280 (Grother, Ngan & Hanaoka, Dec 2019; 189 algorithms from 99 developers; [nvlpubs.nist.gov](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/ir/2019/NIST.IR.8280.pdf)) found false-positive differentials ranging from 10x to over 100x across demographic groups. In one-to-one matching, false positives were highest for West and East African and East Asian faces; in US domestic law-enforcement imagery, American Indian faces showed the highest rates. In one-to-many matching, false positives were highest for African-American women; women showed higher error than men generally. Algorithms developed in China reversed the East-Asian effect — a clear training-data / developer-location proxy. This evidence supports calibrated FR threat education rather than blanket alarmism.

**BLE-tracker stalking is prevalent and vendor detection is imperfect.** PETS 2024 "Please Unstalk Me" (PoPETs 2024 issue 4; [petsymposium.org](https://petsymposium.org/popets/2024/popets-2024-0082.pdf)) combined a survey of N=5,253 with a 200k-install detector app generating roughly 1,400 unwanted-tracking alarms per day. It found 19.13% lifetime stalking prevalence, 8.47% victims of physical tracking, and that 44.28% of stalking victims were also location-tracked, with AirTags most prevalent (Tile and Samsung also widespread). PETS 2023 "Track You" (Shafqat et al.; [par.nsf.gov](https://par.nsf.gov/biblio/10533293-track-you-deep-dive-safety-alerts-apple-airtags)) demonstrated a cloned AirTag that evades Apple's anti-stalking safety alerts and characterized the alert triggers (time, device model, battery, distance). Together these ground a BLE-sweep / awareness module that must honestly disclose its failure modes.

**IMSI-catchers leave detectable anomaly signatures, but low-power attacks can evade detection.** SeaGlass (Ney et al., UW; PoPETs 2017; 15 vehicles across Seattle and Milwaukee; [techpolicylab.uw.edu](https://techpolicylab.uw.edu/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/SeaGlass-Enabling-City-Wide-IMSI-Catcher-Detection.pdf)) and EFF's open-source Crocodile Hunter (4G / srsLTE, USRP B200; [github.com/EFForg/crocodilehunter](https://github.com/EFForg/crocodilehunter)) detect cell-site simulators via tower-identity and signal-strength anomalies and broadcast-message irregularities. However, the research and surrounding community evidence show that low-power attacks can evade signal-anomaly detection, so any Greyline feature in this space must be framed as risk-indication, not certainty.

### Traveler-harm epidemiology

**Road traffic, not terrorism, is the dominant traveler killer — the highest-leverage evidence correction available.** The CDC Yellow Book "Injury and Death During Travel" ([cdc.gov](https://www.cdc.gov/yellow-book/hcp/environmental-hazards-risks/injury-and-death-during-travel.html)), using 2019-2021 US State Department data covering more than 1,500 nonnatural deaths, ranks motor-vehicle crashes #1 at approximately 26% (402 deaths), followed by homicide (292), suicide (225), and drowning (206) — versus only 15 terrorism deaths. Globally, road crashes cause approximately 1.3M deaths per year. Greyline's per-trip kit currently surfaces government advisories (which are terrorism- and crime-weighted) but has no road-safety module; correcting this is the single highest-leverage, evidence-backed change the product can make.

### Data-visualization perception

**Graphical perception has an empirical accuracy ranking that should govern every chart.** Cleveland & McGill (_JASA_ 1984, 79(387):531-554; [tandfonline.com](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/01621459.1984.10478080)) rank elementary perceptual tasks by accuracy: position on a common scale > length > angle/slope > area > shading/color-saturation. This directly dictates Greyline's chart choices — prefer dot and bar charts over pies — and warns that the scratch-map's choropleth shading is the _least_ accurate encoding for quantitative read-off, so it should be paired with framed-rectangle or dot encodings whenever exact magnitudes matter. Combined with Sweller's CLT, it also argues for a low-extraneous-load "data ink" mode on the cockpit home.

### OSINT chronolocation (journalism)

**Sun/shadow geolocation is reproducible physics that validates hypotheses rather than discovering locations.** Bellingcat's "Using the Sun and the Shadows for Geolocation" ([bellingcat.com](https://www.bellingcat.com/resources/2020/12/03/using-the-sun-and-the-shadows-for-geolocation/)) combines a practitioner method with SunCalc and Shadow Finder to date and locate imagery from shadow length and bearing. The rigorous-practice caveat is essential: shadow analysis _validates_ candidate locations, it does not _discover_ them. The underlying solar-ephemeris computation is deterministic and fully bundleable offline (SunCalc-class), grounding a privacy-first journalism verification tool with no network call.

## Open questions

- **Unicity at small-n.** Is implementing the de Montjoye unicity metric defensible at small n? The paper assumes large datasets; for a single user's trip log (tens to hundreds of points) the risk score must be calibrated and explained carefully to avoid false precision — what reference population does the score compare against offline?
- **Web Bluetooth feasibility.** Web Bluetooth scanning is unsupported in Firefox/Safari and gated to secure contexts. Can a local-only Next.js app reliably offer the opt-in BLE-tracker scan, or should it stay a checklist plus OS-tool launcher only?
- **Road-safety dataset selection.** What is the smallest bundleable, license-clean road-safety dataset (WHO GHO vs OECD vs World Bank) that gives per-country death rates plus drive-side / seatbelt / helmet law flags without a network connector?
- **Export-sanitizer defaults.** Should the export sanitizer use k-anonymity, local differential privacy, or a hybrid by default — and what epsilon / k defaults are honest for a privacy-marketed product, given documented reconstruction attacks even on DP mechanisms?
- **OPSEC decision-card liability.** For the OPSEC decision cards, what is the liability / ethics posture of shipping prescriptive "do Y now" counter-surveillance actions, and should they be framed as educational pattern-recognition prompts rather than directives?
- **Dual-use posture.** Does Greyline want to take a position on the dual-use risk of the chrono-geolocation and cell-anomaly tools (the same capabilities aid both a journalist and a stalker), and how should product messaging and any usage notices reflect AGPL / ethical-use norms?
